    Section 2.2 Co-ordinate Geometry

    Co-ordinate geometry connects algebra and geometry. We use coordinates to describe points, and equations to describe lines and circles. The formulae on pages 17â€“19 of your log tables are your toolkit â€” but understanding the properties and anatomy of each formula is what makes them powerful.

    Subsection 2.2.1 The Line

    Before we begin co-ordinate geometry, we review some essential notation and ideas from Junior Cycle. Everything in this section builds on what you already know â€” we are simply making it more precise and more powerful.
    ğŸ”—

    Notation Review (Log Tables p. 17).

    In co-ordinate geometry, we work with points, lines, and segments. The notation matters â€” it tells us exactly what we mean.
    ğŸ”—
    Notice the difference: \(PQ\) extends forever in both directions, \([PQ]\) has endpoints, and \(|PQ|\) is a number â€” a length. Get comfortable reading and writing these. They appear constantly in exam questions and marking schemes.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    What Is a Line?

    A line is a set of points. Every point \((x, y)\) on the line satisfies the equation of that line. We call \((x, y)\) a variable point â€” it can be any point on the line.
    ğŸ”—
    By contrast, \((x_1, y_1)\) is a fixed point â€” a known, specific point on the line. Think of it as an anchor or reference point. Much of co-ordinate geometry is about the relationship between a fixed point and a variable point on the same line.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Slope (Log Tables p. 18).

    Given two points \(P(x_1, y_1)\) and \(Q(x_2, y_2)\text{,}\) the slope of line \(PQ\) is:
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \end{equation*}
    Read this aloud: â€œthe slope of line \(PQ\)â€ â€” not just â€œthe slope of \(P\) and \(Q\)â€. The slope belongs to the line, not to the points.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Anatomy of the Slope Formula.

    Let us decompose the formula. The numerator \(y_2 - y_1\) is the rise â€” the vertical change. The denominator \(x_2 - x_1\) is the run â€” the horizontal change. So slope is:
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{\text{vertical change}}{\text{horizontal change}} \end{equation*}
    Every line segment can be decomposed into a horizontal and a vertical component â€” a supporting triangle. The slope measures how these two components compare.
    ğŸ”—
    This is a relative comparison. The subtraction means we donâ€™t care where the points are in absolute terms â€” only their position relative to each other. A line through \((1, 2)\) and \((3, 6)\) has the same slope as a line through \((101, 102)\) and \((103, 106)\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Properties of Slope.

    Is the slope formula commutative? Yes. It does not matter which point you call \(P\) and which you call \(Q\text{.}\) If you swap them, both the numerator and denominator change sign, and \(\frac{-(y_2-y_1)}{-(x_2-x_1)} = \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1}\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    However, good practice is to let the second point be the one further to the right. This way the run \((x_2 - x_1)\) is positive, and the sign of the slope comes entirely from the rise. This makes it easier to see whether the line is increasing or decreasing:
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Connections to Junior Cycle.

    The slope formula connects to several ideas from the Number strand (JC spec N.1):
    ğŸ”—
    Decomposition: We decompose the change between two points into a vertical part (rise) and a horizontal part (run). This is the same idea as decomposing numbers or operations into simpler parts.
    ğŸ”—
    Commutativity: We saw that the slope formula is commutative with respect to swapping points. This connects directly to the commutative property of multiplication and division of signed numbers (JC spec N.1b).
    ğŸ”—
    Relative comparison: Slope is a ratio. It uses subtraction to compare positions relatively, not absolutely (JC spec N.3a). The actual coordinates donâ€™t matter â€” only the differences do.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Example 2.2.3. Calculating Slope.

    Find the slope of line \(PQ\) where \(P(1, 3)\) and \(Q(4, 9)\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Solution.
    With \(Q\) on the right (\(x_2 = 4 > x_1 = 1\)):
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{align*} m &= \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\\ &= \frac{9 - 3}{4 - 1}\\ &= \frac{6}{3} = 2 \end{align*}
    The rise is \(6\) (positive â€” going up), the run is \(3\) (positive â€” going right). The line is increasing.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Distance (Log Tables p. 18).

    The distance from \(P\) to \(Q\) is:
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} |PQ| = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2} \end{equation*}
    Read this as: â€œthe square root of the sum of two squaresâ€. The two squares are the rise squared and the run squared. This is Pythagoras applied to the supporting triangle.
    ğŸ”—
    Is this formula commutative? Yes â€” and for a stronger reason than slope. Subtraction is not commutative: \(3 - 7 \neq 7 - 3\text{.}\) But squaring removes the sign: \((3-7)^2 = (-4)^2 = 16 = (4)^2 = (7-3)^2\text{.}\) So it genuinely does not matter which point comes first in the distance formula.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Example 2.2.4. Calculating Distance.

    Find \(|PQ|\) where \(P(1, 3)\) and \(Q(4, 9)\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} |PQ| &= \sqrt{(4-1)^2 + (9-3)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{9 + 36}\\ &= \sqrt{45} = 3\sqrt{5} \end{align*}
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Midpoint (Log Tables p. 18).

    The midpoint of \([PQ]\) is:
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2},\; \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right) \end{equation*}
    This is the mean of the \(x\)-coordinates and the mean of the \(y\)-coordinates. The same idea of â€œaverageâ€ that you know from the Statistics strand.
    ğŸ”—
    Notice the crucial difference from slope and distance: midpoint uses addition, not subtraction. This is an absolute comparison â€” where the midpoint is depends on where the actual points are, not just on their relative position. Move both points 10 units to the right and the midpoint moves too.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Equation of a Line (Log Tables p. 18).

    Now we connect slope to the equation of a line. Start with the slope formula, but replace the second point \((x_2, y_2)\) with the variable point \((x, y)\text{:}\)
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} m = \frac{y - y_1}{x - x_1} \end{equation*}
    Multiply both sides by \((x - x_1)\) and we get:
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \end{equation*}
    This is the point-slope formula. It is not named on the log tables, but it is the first equation given under â€œequation of \(PQ\)â€. It says: if you know one fixed point and the slope, you can write the equation of the line.
    ğŸ”—
    The second equation on the log tables is:
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} y = mx + c \end{equation*}
    This is the slope-intercept formula (or better: intercept-slope, because that is the order you see it). Here \(c\) is the \(y\)-intercept â€” where the line crosses the \(y\)-axis. You get this form by expanding the point-slope formula and collecting terms.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Example 2.2.6. Finding the Equation of a Line.

    Find the equation of the line through \((2, 5)\) with slope \(3\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Solution.
    Using the point-slope formula with \((x_1, y_1) = (2, 5)\) and \(m = 3\text{:}\)
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{align*} y - 5 &= 3(x - 2)\\ y - 5 &= 3x - 6\\ y &= 3x - 1 \end{align*}
    In slope-intercept form: \(y = 3x - 1\text{,}\) so the \(y\)-intercept is \(c = -1\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Area of Triangle \(OPQ\) (Log Tables p. 18).

    The area of a triangle with one vertex at the origin \(O\) and the other two at \(P(x_1, y_1)\) and \(Q(x_2, y_2)\) is:
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}|x_1 y_2 - x_2 y_1| \end{equation*}
    Think of it this way: \(x_1 y_2\) and \(x_2 y_1\) are the areas of two rectangles â€” one built from the coordinates of \(P\) stretched to \(Q\text{,}\) and the other from \(Q\) stretched to \(P\text{.}\) The difference gives twice the area of the triangle. The \(\frac{1}{2}\) is there because a triangle is half a parallelogram.

    Checkpoint 2.2.7. Slope Practice.

    Find the slope of line \(AB\) where \(A(-1, 4)\) and \(B(3, -2)\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Hint.
    Let \(B\) be on the right since \(3 > -1\text{.}\) What is the rise? What is the run?
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Answer.
    \(m = -\frac{3}{2}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Solution.
    With \(B\) on the right:
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{align*} m &= \frac{-2 - 4}{3 - (-1)}\\ &= \frac{-6}{4} = -\frac{3}{2} \end{align*}
    The rise is \(-6\) (going down) and the run is \(4\) (going right). The line is decreasing.
