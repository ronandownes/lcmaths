    Section 2.3 Transformation Geometry: Enlargements

    An enlargement is a transformation that changes the size of a figure while keeping its shape. At LC OL, you need to understand how enlargements affect lengths and areas, and be able to solve problems involving them.
    ğŸ”—

    Subsection 2.3.1 Centre of Enlargement and Scale Factor

    An enlargement is defined by two things:
    ğŸ”—
    1. A fixed point called the centre of enlargement.
    ğŸ”—
    2. A number \(k\) called the scale factor, where \(k \in \mathbb{Q}\) and \(k > 0\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    To enlarge a point \(P\) about a centre \(O\) with scale factor \(k\text{:}\) the image \(P'\) lies on the ray \([OP\) such that \(|OP'| = k \times |OP|\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Every point of the figure is transformed this way. Lines from the centre through corresponding points of the object and image are called rays of enlargement.
    ğŸ”—
    Note: when \(0 \lt k \lt 1\text{,}\) we still call it an â€œenlargementâ€ even though the image is smaller. Some texts use the word â€œreductionâ€ for this case.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Subsection 2.3.2 Effect on Lengths

    Under an enlargement with scale factor \(k\text{,}\) every length in the image is \(k\) times the corresponding length in the object:
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \text{image length} = k \times \text{object length} \end{equation*}
    This applies to all lengths: sides, diagonals, perimeters, heights, radii â€” everything.
    ğŸ”—
    Since the shape is preserved and only the size changes, the object and its image are similar figures. All corresponding angles are equal. All corresponding sides are in the ratio \(1 : k\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—

    Example 2.3.2. Lengths Under Enlargement.

    A triangle with sides 6, 8, 10 is enlarged with scale factor \(\frac{3}{2}\text{.}\) Find the side lengths and perimeter of the image.
    ğŸ”—
    Solution.
    Each side is multiplied by \(\frac{3}{2}\text{:}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Sides: \(6 \times \frac{3}{2} = 9\text{,}\) \(8 \times \frac{3}{2} = 12\text{,}\) \(10 \times \frac{3}{2} = 15\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Perimeter of object: \(6 + 8 + 10 = 24\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Perimeter of image: \(9 + 12 + 15 = 36 = \frac{3}{2} \times 24\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    The perimeter also scales by \(k\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Subsection 2.3.3 Effect on Area

    This is the key result for exam questions. Under an enlargement with scale factor \(k\text{:}\)
    ğŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \text{image area} = k^2 \times \text{object area} \end{equation*}
    Why \(k^2\text{?}\) Area depends on two dimensions (e.g. base \(\times\) height). Each dimension is multiplied by \(k\text{,}\) so the area is multiplied by \(k \times k = k^2\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    For example, if a rectangle has base \(b\) and height \(h\text{,}\) its area is \(bh\text{.}\) After enlargement by factor \(k\text{,}\) the base becomes \(kb\) and the height becomes \(kh\text{,}\) so the new area is \((kb)(kh) = k^2 bh\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    This works for any shape â€” triangles, circles, irregular figures â€” because any area can be built up from triangles, and the argument applies to each one.
    ğŸ”—

    Example 2.3.4. Area Under Enlargement.

    A triangle has area \(20\text{ cm}^2\text{.}\) It is enlarged with scale factor \(k = \frac{1}{2}\text{.}\) Find the area of the image.
    ğŸ”—
    Solution.
    Image area \(= k^2 \times \text{object area} = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 \times 20 = \frac{1}{4} \times 20 = 5\text{ cm}^2\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Halving the lengths reduces the area to one quarter.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Example 2.3.5. Finding the Scale Factor from Areas.

    A shape has area \(12\text{ cm}^2\text{.}\) After enlargement, its area is \(108\text{ cm}^2\text{.}\) Find the scale factor.
    ğŸ”—
    Solution.
    \(k^2 = \frac{108}{12} = 9\text{,}\) so \(k = 3\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Example 2.3.6. Enlarging a Circle.

    A circle with radius 4 cm is enlarged with scale factor \(\frac{5}{4}\text{.}\) Find the radius and area of the image.
    ğŸ”—
    Solution.
    New radius \(= \frac{5}{4} \times 4 = 5\) cm.
    ğŸ”—
    Original area \(= \pi(4)^2 = 16\pi\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Image area \(= \left(\frac{5}{4}\right)^2 \times 16\pi = \frac{25}{16} \times 16\pi = 25\pi\) cm\(^2\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Check: \(\pi(5)^2 = 25\pi\text{.}\) Correct.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Subsection 2.3.4 Problem-Solving Exercises

    Checkpoint 2.3.7. Side Lengths and Area.

    A rectangle has dimensions \(6 \times 10\text{.}\) It is enlarged with scale factor \(\frac{2}{3}\text{.}\) Find the dimensions, perimeter, and area of the image.
    ğŸ”—
    Answer.
    Dimensions: \(4 \times \frac{20}{3}\text{.}\) Perimeter: \(\frac{64}{3}\text{.}\) Area: \(\frac{80}{3}\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Solution.
    New dimensions: \(6 \times \frac{2}{3} = 4\) and \(10 \times \frac{2}{3} = \frac{20}{3}\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Perimeter: \(2\left(4 + \frac{20}{3}\right) = 2 \times \frac{32}{3} = \frac{64}{3}\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Area: \(4 \times \frac{20}{3} = \frac{80}{3}\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Check with \(k^2\text{:}\) original area \(= 60\text{;}\) \(\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^2 \times 60 = \frac{4}{9} \times 60 = \frac{240}{9} = \frac{80}{3}\text{.}\) Correct.

    Checkpoint 2.3.10. Map Scales.

    On a map with scale \(1:50\,000\text{,}\) a lake has area \(3.2\text{ cm}^2\text{.}\) Find the actual area of the lake in km\(^2\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Hint.
    The scale factor from map to real life is \(k = 50\,000\text{.}\) Remember: \(100\,000\) cm \(= 1\) km.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Answer.
    \(0.8\text{ km}^2\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Solution.
    Real area \(= k^2 \times \text{map area} = (50\,000)^2 \times 3.2 = 2.5 \times 10^9 \times 3.2 = 8 \times 10^9 \text{ cm}^2\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Convert: \(1\text{ km} = 100\,000\text{ cm}\text{,}\) so \(1\text{ km}^2 = 10^{10}\text{ cm}^2\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Real area \(= \frac{8 \times 10^9}{10^{10}} = 0.8 \text{ km}^2\text{.}\)
