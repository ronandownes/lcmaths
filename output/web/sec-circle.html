    Subsection 2.4.2 What is a Circle?

    In primary school you thought of a circle as a round shape. Now we need a precise definition â€” one that tells us exactly which points belong to the circle and which do not.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 2.4.2. Circle (Appendix B, Definition 40).

    A circle is the set of all points in a plane that are at a given distance (its radius) from a fixed point (its centre).
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Appreciate what this definition says: a circle is not the filled-in region â€” it is only the boundary. The filled-in region is called the disc. Every point on the circle is exactly the same distance from the centre. This idea of equidistance is the key property of the circle and underlies everything we do in this section.
    ðŸ”—
    Think of it this way: if you tie a piece of string to a pin stuck in a board and pull the string taut with a pencil, every point the pencil can reach traces out a circle. The pin is the centre, and the length of the string is the radius.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 2.4.3 Parts of a Circle

    Identify the following terms. Each is defined precisely in Appendix B (Definition 40 and surrounding material) of the specification.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 2.4.3. Circle Terminology.

    Let \(s\) be a circle with centre \(O\) and radius \(r\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    A radius (plural: radii) is any line segment joining the centre to a point on the circle. Its length is \(r\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    A chord is a line segment joining any two points on the circle.
    ðŸ”—
    A diameter is a chord that passes through the centre. Every diameter has length \(2r\text{.}\) This number is also called the diameter of the circle.
    ðŸ”—
    Two points \(A\) and \(B\) on a circle divide it into two pieces called arcs. A semicircle is an arc whose endpoints are the endpoints of a diameter.
    ðŸ”—
    A sector is the region enclosed by an arc and the two radii to its endpoints (like a "slice of pizza").
    ðŸ”—
    The disc is the region inside the circle. Its area is \(\pi r^2\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    The circumference is the total length around the circle: \(C = 2\pi r = \pi d\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 2.4.4. Tangent to a Circle (Appendix B, Definition 42).

    A line \(l\) is called a tangent to a circle \(s\) when \(l\) meets \(s\) in exactly one point. That point is called the point of contact.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 2.4.5. Tangentâ€“Radius Theorem (Appendix B, Theorem 20).

    Each tangent is perpendicular to the radius drawn to the point of contact.
    ðŸ”—
    Conversely, if \(P\) lies on a circle and a line through \(P\) is perpendicular to the radius at \(P\text{,}\) then that line is a tangent.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    This theorem is extremely important for exam questions that ask you to find the equation of a tangent. The method is: find the slope of the radius, then the tangent slope is the negative reciprocal.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection 2.4.5 The Circle on the Coordinate Plane

    We now connect the geometric definition to algebra. Recall the definition: a circle is the set of all points at distance \(r\) from a centre \((h, k)\text{.}\) If \((x, y)\) is any point on the circle, the distance from \((x, y)\) to \((h, k)\) equals \(r\text{.}\) Using the distance formula:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} \sqrt{(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2} = r \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Squaring both sides gives us the standard equation.
    ðŸ”—

    Equation of a Circle.

    The circle with centre \((h, k)\) and radius \(r\) has equation:
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} (x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Special case: When the centre is the origin \((0, 0)\text{:}\)
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + y^2 = r^2 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Appreciate that this equation is simply the distance formula in disguise. Every point \((x, y)\) satisfying the equation is exactly \(r\) units from the centre. This is entirely consistent with Definition 40.
    ðŸ”—

    Remark 2.4.10. Reading an Equation.

    Given an equation like \((x - 3)^2 + (y + 4)^2 = 49\text{,}\) you can immediately identify the centre as \((3, -4)\) and the radius as \(\sqrt{49} = 7\text{.}\) Watch the signs carefully: \((y + 4)\) means \(k = -4\text{.}\)
