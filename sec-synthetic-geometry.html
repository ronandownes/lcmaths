    Section 2.1 Synthetic Geometry

    Geometry is one of the oldest branches of mathematics â€” the ancient Greeks built it up from a handful of basic ideas into an extraordinary logical structure. In this section, we follow that same path. We start with a few terms so basic they cannot be defined, state a few truths so fundamental they cannot be proved, and then build everything else from there.
    ğŸ”—
    At Leaving Certificate Ordinary Level, you are expected to solve problems using the theory. No proofs are examinable. But understanding the logical structure â€” what follows from what â€” is what makes the difference between memorising and truly knowing geometry.

    Subsection 2.1.2 The Building Blocks

    From our undefined terms, we immediately get our first definitions and our first axiom.
    ğŸ”—

    Axiom 2.1.3. Axiom 1 â€” Two Points.

    There is exactly one line through any two given points.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    This is beautifully simple. Pick any two points â€” there is exactly one line through them. Not zero, not two. Exactly one. We write \(AB\) for the line through \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—

    Definition 2.1.5. Segment.

    The line segment \([AB]\) is the part of line \(AB\) between \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) including both endpoints.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Definition 2.1.6. Ray.

    The ray \([AB\) starts at \(A\) and passes through \(B\text{,}\) extending forever beyond \(B\text{.}\) The point \(A\) is the endpoint of the ray.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Definition 2.1.7. Collinear Points.

    Three or more points are collinear if they all lie on a single line.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Definition 2.1.8. Triangle.

    If \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) \(C\) are three non-collinear points, the triangle \(\triangle ABC\) is the figure enclosed by the segments \([AB]\text{,}\) \([BC]\text{,}\) and \([CA]\text{.}\) The segments are its sides and the points are its vertices.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Distance.

    We write \(|AB|\) for the distance between points \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\) This is also the length of segment \([AB]\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Axiom 2.1.9. Axiom 2 â€” Ruler.
    Distance has these properties:
    ğŸ”—
    1. \(|AB| \geq 0\) (distance is never negative).
    ğŸ”—
    2. \(|AB| = |BA|\) (same distance either way).
    ğŸ”—
    3. If \(C\) lies on \(AB\) between \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\) then \(|AB| = |AC| + |CB|\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    4. Given any ray from \(A\) and any number \(k \geq 0\text{,}\) there is exactly one point on the ray at distance \(k\) from \(A\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Property 3 is the one you use constantly: if a point is between two others, the distances add up. Property 4 says you can always â€œmark offâ€ a distance â€” essential for constructions.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—

    Definition 2.1.10. Midpoint.

    The midpoint \(M\) of segment \([AB]\) is the point on the segment with \(|AM| = |MB| = \frac{|AB|}{2}\text{.}\)

    ğŸ”—

    Two triangles are congruent if all six measurements match: three sides and three angles. But you do not need all six â€” three carefully chosen measurements are enough.

    Two triangles are congruent if:

    SAS: Two sides and the included angle are equal, or
    ğŸ”—
    ASA: Two angles and the included side are equal, or

    SSS: All three sides are equal.

    ğŸ”—

    Subsection 2.1.7 Sides and Angles in a Triangle (Theorems 7 and 8)

    Now we reach new territory for LC OL. These two theorems connect the sizes of sides and angles within a single triangle.
    ğŸ”—

    Theorem 2.1.44. Theorem 7 â€” Bigger Side, Bigger Angle.

    (1) In \(\triangle ABC\text{,}\) if \(|AC| \gt |AB|\text{,}\) then \(|\angle ABC| \gt |\angle ACB|\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    In words: the angle opposite the longer side is the larger angle.
    ğŸ”—
    (2) Conversely, the side opposite the larger angle is the longer side.
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Proof idea: Place a point \(D\) on \([AC]\) with \(|AD| = |AB|\text{.}\) Then \(\triangle ABD\) is isosceles, and combining the Exterior Angle Theorem with the Isosceles Triangle Theorem gives \(|\angle ACB| \lt |\angle ABC|\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Think of it physically: a longer side needs to â€œpushâ€ the opposite angle wider to make room.
    ğŸ”—

    Theorem 2.1.46. Theorem 8 â€” Triangle Inequality.

    Any two sides of a triangle are together greater than the third: \(|AB| + |BC| \gt |AC|\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    This is the geometric way of saying: the shortest path between two points is a straight line. Any detour via a third point makes the journey longer.

    ğŸ”—
